Color on screens is light. Color on paper is pigment. This distinction, which sounds merely technical, changes almost everything about how designers should think about color in digital contexts.

Pigment-based color works subtractively — mixing colors removes wavelengths. Light-based color works additively — mixing colors adds wavelengths. The result is that the same color can look fundamentally different depending on the medium, and the vocabulary of traditional color theory requires careful translation.

## The Screen Diversity Problem

Every screen renders color differently. Different panel technologies, different backlights, different gamma settings, different ambient lighting conditions — all of these affect how a color appears to the person viewing it. A blue chosen carefully on a calibrated design workstation will shift on an uncalibrated consumer laptop and shift further on a phone screen in direct sunlight.

This is not a problem with a clean solution. It is a condition that designers must work within. The practical response is to design in ranges rather than absolutes. To test on multiple devices. To ensure that your color choices communicate their intent across a variety of rendering conditions, not just the one in which they were selected.

## Accessibility as Constraint and Discipline

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines establish minimum contrast ratios for text and interface elements. These are sometimes experienced as constraints — things you can't do because accessibility requirements prohibit them.

But the designer who treats accessibility requirements as a discipline rather than a constraint arrives somewhere more interesting. The requirement to maintain a 4.5:1 contrast ratio between text and background turns out to be a useful guardrail against choices that would have been hard to read anyway. Designing for users with visual impairments often produces designs that are clearer for everyone.

> Constraints clarify. The best design work often happens within tight limits, not in spite of them.

## Dark Mode and the Changing Default

The rise of dark mode has introduced a new layer of complexity. A color palette designed for light backgrounds does not automatically invert to work on dark backgrounds. Semantic color systems — where color carries meaning rather than mere aesthetic value — become more important as interfaces need to function in multiple contexts.

The most robust digital color systems define colors by their role (primary, secondary, destructive, success) rather than by their appearance. When the appearance needs to change for dark mode, the role remains constant and the implementation changes.

This is a higher level of abstraction than most color decisions historically required. But it is the right level of abstraction for systems that need to be coherent across platforms, modes, and devices that designers cannot fully anticipate.
