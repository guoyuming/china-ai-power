Iceland in June operates on a different logic. The sun describes a low arc across the horizon at midnight and the land glows amber in a way that makes everything look slightly unreal — a landscape rendered in the palette of a dusk that never fully arrives.

We flew into Reykjavik on a Tuesday and spent the first day doing what everyone does: walking the compact city center, visiting Hallgrimskirkja, eating lamb soup in a restaurant where the windows look out on a street that was, two hundred years ago, a fishing settlement of turf houses.

## Day Two: South Coast

The ring road south of Reykjavik delivers quickly on the promise of the landscape. Within an hour of leaving the city, the terrain had shifted from suburban flatness to something geological and violent. Waterfalls visible from the highway. Mountains that appear to have been folded by enormous pressure. Farmhouses sitting implausibly close to cliffs.

Seljalandsfoss is the waterfall you can walk behind. This sounds like a minor attraction and is, in fact, quite extraordinary — the fall is wide and heavy enough to create its own weather, and the cave behind it smells of cold rock and perpetual moisture. We came out the other side wet and briefly disoriented.

## The Interior

Most visitors stay on the ring road. The interior of Iceland is accessible by 4WD vehicle on F-roads that are closed for most of the year. We took the Kjölur route on day four, a track through a highland desert of gravel and volcanic ash that runs between two glaciers.

The landscape is not beautiful in any conventional sense. It is too austere for that word. It is strange in a way that demands sustained attention. Nothing grows that does not have to. The wind is constant.

> There is a kind of place that does not accommodate human presence so much as tolerate it, and the Icelandic interior is one of those places.

## What Remains

A week is not enough to understand Iceland, but it is enough to understand why people return. The light. The absolute clarity of the air at altitude. The sense that the geology is still in motion, that the ground beneath you is not finished becoming what it will eventually be.

We drove north on the last day along the east fjords, where the road traces the edge of the land and the water below is impossibly still and dark. It was past eleven at night. The sun was still above the mountains.
